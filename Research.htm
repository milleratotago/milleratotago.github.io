Research Topics

  • Cognitive psychology: Simple human information processing mechanisms, including perception, attention, decision making, memory retrieval, mental rotation, and motor control. Some specific issues:
    • Multi-tasking. Why does performance suffer when we try to do more than one task at the same time? References.
    • Discrete versus continuous models of human information processing. Can we start to select an appropriate response before we have finished perceiving a stimulus, or does perception have to finish before response selection can begin? References.
    • Redundancy gain. What happens when we try to attend to two stimuli at the same time. Can we really do that, or do we actually attend to one at a time, perhaps switching back and forth mentally? References.
    • Selective attention. What happens when we attend to one stimulus and ignore another? What sort of "attentional emphasis" allows us to be more aware of the attended one than the unattended? And how much do we process the one we arenot attending to? Do we process it more than we consciously know? References.
    • Subliminal perception. When stimuli are presented so briefly or so weakly that we are not consciously aware of them, might we still perceive them a little bit without awareness? References.
    • Probability effects. How does sensorimotor decision making depend on stimulus and response probability? References.
    • Motor processes. How are motor areas of the brain activated by cognitive processes, and vice versa? References.
    • Other. Studies that don't fit neatly into one of the above categories. References.
  • Quantitative and statistical models and methods: Whenever I can, I try to develop quantitative models of the phenomena I'm looking at. In addition, I like to look at the statistical methods used in the field, to see whether they might have flaws or shortcoming that can be improved upon. So, I've investigated things like:
    • Reaction time models. Here, the idea is to develop a quantitative model that can describe reaction time in a set of related information processing tasks. References.
    • Statistical issues. Methods of statistical analysis that seem quite reasonable sometimes have unexpected consequences that could contaminate the interpretations of the results. Thus, I use computer simulations to see whether certain statistical methods give the "right" answers in the long run, or whether they consistently err in a given direction. References.
    • Software. Related free software. References.
